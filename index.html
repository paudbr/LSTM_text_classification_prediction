Overview

What does this
project do?

Taylor Swift is one of the most prolific singer-songwriters of her generation. With 11 studio albums, each with a distinctly different lyrical and musical style, her discography forms a rich dataset for Natural Language Processing.

This project explores whether machine learning models can learn to distinguish between albums based on lyrics alone — and whether they can generate new lyrics that sound like Taylor Swift.

We implemented and compared several NLP approaches, from the probabilistic simplicity of Naive Bayes to the sequential memory of LSTM networks.

Text Preprocessing

From raw lyrics to
numerical vectors

STEP 01

Stopword Removal

English stopwords (NLTK) are removed — articles, prepositions, pronouns. Only meaningful content words remain.

STEP 02

Lowercase & Clean

All text is lowercased and non-alphanumeric characters are stripped, producing a uniform, clean string.

STEP 03

Tokenization

The cleaned string is split into individual word tokens using NLTK's word_tokenize.

STEP 04

Porter Stemmer

Each token is reduced to its root form — "loves", "loving", "loved" all become "love" — reducing vocabulary size.

STEP 05

CountVectorizer

Tokens are converted to sparse numerical vectors (5,000-word vocabulary). Each lyric chunk becomes a row of word counts.

STEP 06

Lyric Chunking

Full lyrics are split into 35-word chunks (shuffled). This multiplies training samples and prevents memorisation.

# Before → 1 sample per song # After → ~20 chunks per song chunk_lyrics(lyrics, size=35)

Classification Models

Four approaches,
one question

Best Result

Multinomial NB + Bagging

Probabilistic classifier based on Bayes' theorem. Word frequencies per album are used to compute posterior probabilities. Bagging further reduces variance.

Accuracy66.0%
Deep Learning

LSTM Classification

Embedding layer → LSTM → Dropout(10%) → Dense(softmax). Reaches 100% training accuracy but test accuracy matches NB, at much greater cost.

Accuracy64.0%
Baseline

Multinomial NB (11 classes)

Initial model with all 11 album labels. Albums with few songs (debut, folklore, evermore) dragged down overall performance.

Accuracy63.5%
Comparison

SVM

Support Vector Machine on the same 9-class reduced dataset. Despite its theoretical advantages for text, it underperformed Naive Bayes here.

Key Findings

What we learned

🧹

Preprocessing is critical

Stopword removal and stemming are essential for reducing noise. Generic emotional words ("love", "know", "never") appear across all albums and naturally limit classification accuracy.

⚖️

Simple models compete

Multinomial NB achieves nearly identical accuracy to LSTM classification, with a fraction of the computational cost. Complexity doesn't always win.

📊

Data imbalance matters

Albums with more songs (re-recorded Taylor's Versions include vault tracks) consistently show higher per-class recall. The debut album performed poorly due to too few training samples.

🏷️

Label engineering helps

Merging lyrically similar albums (folklore + evermore → folkmore) and dropping the debut improved overall accuracy and reduced confusion between adjacent labels.

🔁

LSTM generation is coherent

At 50 epochs, the TensorFlow LSTM generates text that follows Taylor Swift's lyrical patterns, though it sometimes produces surreal or nonsensical phrases.

🎯

Room for improvement

Artist-specific stop words, TF-IDF weighting, pre-trained embeddings, and more training epochs could all improve both classification and generation quality.
